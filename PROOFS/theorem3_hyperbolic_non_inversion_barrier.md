# Theorem 3 — Hyperbolic Non-Inversion Barrier Theorem

This theorem formalizes the central LAAMO claim that the inversion boundary is
at infinite geometric distance and therefore cannot be crossed by finite-energy
geodesic or safeguarded descent trajectories. The statement is written to match
the positive-Jacobian checks, quality filters, and smoothing safeguards in
`FEM/mesh_adaptation.hpp` and the transport logic in `FEM/state_transport.hpp`.

## 1. Admissible domain and barrier coordinate

Let $J$ denote the local Jacobian or deformation gradient associated with a
mesh element or transported state. Fix a barrier threshold $\epsilon>0$ and
define the admissible domain
$$\mathcal D_\epsilon := \{J\in GL^+(3): \det(J)>\epsilon\}.$$

Introduce the scalar barrier coordinate
$$\tau(J):=\det(J)-\epsilon,$$
so that the boundary of admissibility is
$$\partial\mathcal D_\epsilon = \{J:\tau(J)=0\}.$$

Define the barrier potential
$$\Phi(J):=-\log\tau(J)=-\log(\det(J)-\epsilon).$$

Then $\Phi$ is smooth on $\mathcal D_\epsilon$ and satisfies
$$\lim_{\tau(J)\downarrow 0}\Phi(J)=+\infty.$$
This is the singular barrier that encodes mesh inversion as an infinite-cost
event.

## 2. Barrier-induced Riemannian metric

To formalize the geometric distance-to-boundary claim, equip $\mathcal D_\epsilon$
with the barrier-weighted Riemannian metric
$$g_J(U,V) := \langle U,V\rangle_F + \lambda\,\frac{D\tau(J)[U]D\tau(J)[V]}{\tau(J)^2},$$
where $\langle U,V\rangle_F := \operatorname{tr}(U^TV)$ is the Frobenius inner
product, $\lambda>0$ is fixed, and $D\tau(J)[U]$ is the directional derivative
of the determinant coordinate.

This metric has two essential features:
- the base Frobenius term keeps the metric positive definite on all admissible
  tangent directions,
- the barrier term blows up as $\tau(J)\downarrow 0$, forcing the inversion
  boundary to lie at infinite distance.

## 3A Continuous geodesic barrier

The following subsections treat the continuous geometric statements (geodesics,
barrier metric, and infinite-distance claims). A separate discrete timestep
positivity result is given in Section 3B to ensure the numerical updates
preserve admissibility under a small-step condition.

## 3. Positivity and regularity of the metric

### Lemma 3.1
For every $J\in\mathcal D_\epsilon$ and every nonzero tangent direction $U$,
$$g_J(U,U)>0.$$

### Proof
The Frobenius term gives $\langle U,U\rangle_F>0$ for $U\neq 0$. The barrier
term is nonnegative because it is a square divided by $\tau(J)^2>0$. Hence
$$g_J(U,U)=\langle U,U\rangle_F + \lambda\frac{D\tau(J)[U]^2}{\tau(J)^2}>0,$$
which proves positive definiteness.

The metric is smooth on $\mathcal D_\epsilon$ because both $J\mapsto\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle_F$
and $J\mapsto \tau(J)^{-2}$ are smooth where $\tau(J)>0$.

## 4. Infinite-distance-to-boundary theorem

### Lemma 3.2
If a $C^1$ curve $J:[0,1)\to\mathcal D_\epsilon$ satisfies
$$\tau(J(s))\downarrow 0\quad\text{as }s\uparrow 1,$$
then its $g$-length is infinite:
$$L_g(J)=\int_0^1 \sqrt{g_{J(s)}(\dot J(s),\dot J(s))}\,ds=+\infty.$$

### Proof
Since $g_J$ dominates its barrier component,
\begin{align*}
\sqrt{g_{J(s)}(\dot J(s),\dot J(s))}
&\ge \sqrt{\lambda}\,\frac{|D\tau(J(s))[\dot J(s)]|}{\tau(J(s))} \\
&= \sqrt{\lambda}\,\frac{|\frac{d}{ds}\tau(J(s))|}{\tau(J(s))}.
\end{align*}
Therefore
\begin{align*}
L_g(J)
&\ge \sqrt{\lambda}\int_0^1 \frac{|\tau'(s)|}{\tau(s)}\,ds \\
&\ge \sqrt{\lambda}\left|\int_0^1 \frac{\tau'(s)}{\tau(s)}\,ds\right| \\
&= \sqrt{\lambda}\,\big|\log\tau(J(s))\big|\Big|_{s=0}^{s\uparrow1}.
\end{align*}
Since $\tau(J(s))\downarrow 0$, we have $\log\tau(J(s))\to -\infty$, hence
the length diverges to $+\infty$.

This is the precise infinite-distance statement: the inversion boundary is not
merely hard to reach, it is metrically infinitely far away.

## 5. Geodesic non-crossing theorem

### Theorem 3
Let $J(s)$ be a geodesic of the barrier metric $g$ or a trajectory generated by
an energy functional that includes the barrier term $\Phi$ with finite initial
energy. If $J(0)\in\mathcal D_\epsilon$, then
$$\tau(J(s))>0\qquad\text{for all }s$$
and therefore
$$\det(J(s))>\epsilon\qquad\text{for all }s.$$
In particular, geodesic trajectories cannot cross the inversion boundary
$\det(J)=\epsilon$.

### Proof
Assume by contradiction that a geodesic $J(s)$ reaches the boundary at some
finite parameter value $s_*$, i.e. $\tau(J(s))\downarrow 0$ as $s\uparrow s_*$. 
Then by Lemma 3.2 the $g$-length from the initial point to the boundary is
infinite. But geodesics of finite energy and finite initial length cannot reach
points at infinite distance in finite parameter time. This contradiction proves
that the boundary is unreachable.

For energy-based descent dynamics, the same conclusion follows from the barrier
blow-up of $\Phi$: if a continuous trajectory satisfied bounded barrier energy,
then any approach to $\tau=0$ would force $\Phi\to+\infty$, contradicting the
finite-energy hypothesis.

## 6. Hessian interpretation

The barrier potential gives the scalar second derivative
$$\Phi''(\tau)=\frac{1}{\tau^2},$$
which is strictly positive on $\mathcal D_\epsilon$ and diverges as
$\tau\downarrow 0$. In the determinant coordinate, the barrier metric is
therefore asymptotically hyperbolic: the conformal factor is exactly the one
that produces logarithmic distance to the boundary. This is the geometric
mechanism behind the non-inversion property.

The barrier is analogous to logarithmic self-concordant barriers used in
interior-point optimization: its Hessian scale and logarithmic growth near the
boundary place it in the same geometric class as classical self-concordant
logarithmic barriers.

## 3B Discrete timestep positivity preservation

The continuous infinite-distance result does not automatically imply that a
discrete timestep or update will preserve positivity; numerical updates must be
controlled. The following theorem makes the required small-step condition
explicit.

### Theorem 3B (Discrete positivity preservation)
Assume $J_k\in\mathcal D_\epsilon$ and the update takes the additive form
$$J_{k+1}=J_k+\Delta J_k.$$ 
On any bounded admissible compact set there exists a constant $C_{\det}>0$ such
that for all admissible $J$ and perturbations $E$ one has the determinant
perturbation bound
$$|\det(J+E)-\det(J)|\le C_{\det}\,\|E\|.$$ 
If the timestep/update satisfies the small-step condition
$$\|\Delta J_k\| \le \theta\frac{\tau(J_k)}{C_{\det}}$$
for some fixed $0<\theta<1$, then
$$\det(J_{k+1}) \ge \det(J_k) - C_{\det}\|\Delta J_k\| \ge \epsilon + (1-\theta)\tau(J_k) > \epsilon,$$
so admissibility is preserved and the iterate remains inside $\mathcal D_\epsilon$.

### Proof
The determinant perturbation bound gives
$$\det(J_{k+1})\ge \det(J_k) - C_{\det}\|\Delta J_k\|.$$ 
Using the small-step condition yields
$$\det(J_{k+1})\ge \det(J_k) - C_{\det}\cdot\theta\frac{\tau(J_k)}{C_{\det}} = \det(J_k)-\theta\tau(J_k)=\epsilon+(1-\theta)\tau(J_k),$$
which is strictly greater than $\epsilon$ because $\theta<1$ and $\tau(J_k)>0$.
Thus the update preserves positivity.

## 7. Code-level interpretation

The mesh-quality logic in `FEM/mesh_adaptation.hpp` enforces positive signed
volume, positive scaled Jacobian, and minimum quality thresholds. These are the
discrete analogues of the barrier domain $\mathcal D_\epsilon$. The smoothing
and refinement operators are therefore allowed to move within the admissible
region, but the barrier theorem guarantees that the inversion boundary is at
infinite metric distance and cannot be crossed by a finite-energy update.

## 8. Final thesis-ready statement

For the barrier potential
$$\Phi(J)=-\log(\det(J)-\epsilon),$$
the induced barrier metric
$$g_J(U,V)=\langle U,V\rangle_F+\lambda\frac{D\tau(J)[U]D\tau(J)[V]}{\tau(J)^2}$$
is positive definite on the admissible domain $\mathcal D_\epsilon$ and places
the inversion boundary at infinite distance. Hence any finite-energy geodesic,
line-search trajectory, or trust-region update that remains inside the
admissible set cannot cross into $\det(J)=\epsilon$.

### Literature positioning
The barrier construction and infinite-distance argument connect to the
interior-point and barrier-function literature; the logarithmic barrier is
analogous to self-concordant logarithmic barriers widely used in optimization
theory. This situates the non-inversion result where geometric analysis and
optimization-based barrier methods meet.

### Future direction: geodesic completeness and curvature analysis
The barrier metric also suggests a stronger differential-geometric theorem: the
metric completion of $(\mathcal D_\epsilon,g)$ should coincide with the closure
of the admissible set with the inversion boundary placed at infinite distance,
so that the manifold is geodesically complete in the sense of Hopf–Rinow. A
full proof would combine the barrier blow-up near $\det(J)=\epsilon$ with the
global Frobenius control that prevents escape to infinity in finite length.

The same metric invites curvature analysis. Near the boundary, the conformal
factor $\tau(J)^{-2}$ produces an asymptotically hyperbolic regime: sectional
curvatures are expected to become negatively curved in directions with nonzero
determinant variation, while directions tangent to the level sets of $\tau$
remain dominated by the Frobenius term. In particular, the metric degenerates
only through the barrier factor as $\tau\downarrow0$, which is precisely the
mechanism behind the infinite-distance property. A complete curvature theorem
would compute the sectional curvature tensor of $g$ and show that the boundary
is asymptotically hyperbolic with controlled curvature blow-up, placing the
result in the same geometric class as complete barrier metrics in nonlinear
optimization and geometric analysis.
